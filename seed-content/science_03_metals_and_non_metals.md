# Metals and Non-metals - Learning Path

## 1. Concept Introduction

### What are Metals and Non-metals?

Elements around us are classified into two main categories: **Metals** and **Non-metals** based on their physical and chemical properties.

**Everyday Examples:**
- 🔩 **Metals:** Iron nails, Aluminium foil, Gold jewellery, Copper wires
- 💎 **Non-metals:** Coal, Sulphur powder, Oxygen in air, Graphite in pencils
- ⚡ **Metalloids:** Silicon chips, Germanium in electronics

### Classification of Elements:

```
ELEMENTS
├── METALS (80% of all elements)
│   ├── Highly Reactive: Na, K, Ca, Mg
│   ├── Moderately Reactive: Zn, Fe, Pb
│   ├── Less Reactive: Cu, Hg, Ag
│   └── Noble Metals: Au, Pt
├── NON-METALS
│   ├── Solids: C, S, P, I
│   ├── Liquids: Br
│   └── Gases: O₂, N₂, H₂, Cl₂
└── METALLOIDS (8 elements)
    └── B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te, Po, At
```

**Key Components:**

| Property | Metals | Non-metals |
|----------|--------|------------|
| **Lustre** | Shiny (metallic lustre) | Dull (except iodine) |
| **Hardness** | Hard (except Na, K) | Soft (except diamond) |
| **Malleability** | Can be beaten into sheets | Not malleable |
| **Ductility** | Can be drawn into wires | Not ductile |
| **Conductivity** | Good conductors | Poor conductors (except graphite) |
| **Sonority** | Sonorous (ringing sound) | Not sonorous |
| **State at RT** | Solid (except Hg) | Solid, Liquid, Gas |

## 3. Concept Explanations

### 3.1 Physical Properties of Metals

Metals have characteristic physical properties that distinguish them from non-metals.

#### Important Properties:

**1. Metallic Lustrе:**
- Metals have a shining surface
- Can be polished to make them more lustrous
- Example: Gold, Silver, Aluminium

**2. Hardness:**
- Metals are generally hard
- Exception: Sodium and Potassium can be cut with knife

**3. Malleability:**
- Ability to be beaten into thin sheets
- Gold and Silver are most malleable
- Example: Aluminium foil for food packaging

**4. Ductility:**
- Ability to be drawn into thin wires
- Gold is most ductile (1g can make 2km wire!)

**5. Conductivity:**
- Good conductors of heat and electricity
- Silver and Copper are best conductors
- Lead and Mercury are poor conductors

**6. Sonority:**
- Metals produce ringing sound when struck
- Used in musical instruments and bells

```
METAL STRUCTURE
    ● ● ● ● ●
    ● ● ● ● ●
    ● ● ● ● ●
    
Layers can slide over each other
→ Malleability and Ductility
```

### 3.2 Chemical Properties of Metals

#### Reaction with Oxygen (Formation of Oxides):

Metals react with oxygen to form metal oxides.

**General Equation:**
$$ \text{Metal} + \text{Oxygen} \rightarrow \text{Metal Oxide} $$

**Examples:**

1. **Sodium:**
$$ 4\text{Na}_{(s)} + \text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2\text{Na}_2\text{O}_{(s)} $$

2. **Magnesium:**
$$ 2\text{Mg}_{(s)} + \text{O}_{2(g)} \xrightarrow{\text{heat}} 2\text{MgO}_{(s)} $$

3. **Aluminium:**
$$ 4\text{Al}_{(s)} + 3\text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2\text{Al}_2\text{O}_{3(s)} $$

4. **Iron:**
$$ 3\text{Fe}_{(s)} + 2\text{O}_{2(g)} \rightarrow \text{Fe}_3\text{O}_{4(s)} $$

**Nature of Metal Oxides:**
- Generally **basic** in nature
- Turn red litmus blue
- Some are amphoteric (Al₂O₃, ZnO)

---

#### Reaction with Water:

Metals react with water to form metal hydroxides or oxides and hydrogen gas.

**Reactivity Order with Water:**

```
MOST REACTIVE
    ↓
K  → Reacts violently with cold water
Na → Reacts vigorously with cold water
Ca → Reacts less violently with cold water
Mg → Reacts with hot water
Al → Reacts with steam
Zn → Reacts with steam
Fe → Reacts with steam (slowly)
    ↓
LEAST REACTIVE
```

**Examples:**

1. **Sodium with cold water:**
$$ 2\text{Na}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} + \text{Heat} $$

2. **Magnesium with hot water:**
$$ \text{Mg}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{O}_{(g)} \rightarrow \text{MgO}_{(s)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} $$

3. **Iron with steam:**
$$ 3\text{Fe}_{(s)} + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(g)} \rightarrow \text{Fe}_3\text{O}_{4(s)} + 4\text{H}_{2(g)} $$

**Metals that DON'T react with water:**
- Lead, Copper, Silver, Gold

---

#### Reaction with Acids:

Metals react with dilute acids to form salt and hydrogen gas.

**General Equation:**
$$ \text{Metal} + \text{Acid} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Hydrogen gas} $$

**Examples:**

1. **With Hydrochloric Acid:**
$$ \text{Zn}_{(s)} + 2\text{HCl}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnCl}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} $$

2. **With Sulphuric Acid:**
$$ \text{Mg}_{(s)} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{MgSO}_{4(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} $$

3. **With Nitric Acid:**
- Most metals don't evolve H₂ with HNO₃
- HNO₃ is a strong oxidizing agent
- Exception: Mg and Mn evolve H₂ with very dilute HNO₃

**Reactivity Order with Acids:**
```
Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > (H) > Cu > Ag > Au
(React vigorously)    (No reaction)
```

---

#### Reaction with Bases:

Some metals react with bases (alkalis) to form salt and hydrogen gas.

**Example - Zinc with Sodium Hydroxide:**
$$ \text{Zn}_{(s)} + 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Na}_2\text{ZnO}_{2(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} $$

(Sodium zincate is formed)

**Example - Aluminium with Sodium Hydroxide:**
$$ 2\text{Al}_{(s)} + 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaAlO}_{2(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)} $$

(Sodium aluminate is formed)

### 3.3 Reactivity Series

The **Reactivity Series** is the arrangement of metals in decreasing order of their reactivity.

```
REACTIVITY SERIES

K  ──┐
Na   │
Ca   │  React vigorously with cold water
Mg   │
     │
Al   │
Zn   │  React with steam
Fe   │
     │
Pb   │  React slowly with acids
(H)  │  ← Hydrogen reference
     │
Cu   │
Hg   │  Do not react with water or dilute acids
Ag   │
Au   ──┘  (Noble metals - least reactive)

Mnemonic: "Please Stop Calling Me A Careless Zebra Instead Try Learning How Copper Saves Gold"
```

#### Applications of Reactivity Series:

1. **Predicting Displacement Reactions:**
   - More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal
   - Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu ✓
   - Example: Cu + ZnSO₄ → No reaction ✗

2. **Extraction of Metals:**
   - Highly reactive metals extracted by electrolysis
   - Moderately reactive by reduction with carbon
   - Less reactive by heating alone

3. **Corrosion Prevention:**
   - More reactive metal used as sacrificial anode
   - Example: Zinc coating on iron (galvanization)

### 3.4 Occurrence and Extraction of Metals

#### Minerals and Ores:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Mineral** | Natural element/compound in earth's crust | Bauxite, Haematite |
| **Ore** | Mineral from which metal can be extracted economically | Bauxite (Al), Haematite (Fe) |
| **Gangue** | Earthly impurities in ore | Sand, clay, soil |

#### Steps in Extraction:

```
EXTRACTION PROCESS

1. ENRICHMENT OF ORE
   └── Removal of gangue (crushing, washing, magnetic separation)

2. EXTRACTION OF METAL
   └── Reduction of metal oxide to metal

3. REFINING OF METAL
   └── Purification (electrolytic refining)
```

#### Extraction Based on Reactivity:

**Highly Reactive Metals (K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al):**
- Extracted by **electrolysis of molten ore**
- Cannot be reduced by carbon

**Moderately Reactive Metals (Zn, Fe, Pb):**
- Extracted by **reduction with carbon** (coke)
- Metal oxide + C → Metal + CO/CO₂

**Less Reactive Metals (Cu, Hg, Ag):**
- Extracted by **heating alone**
- Metal sulphide heated in air

---

#### Refining of Metals:

**Electrolytic Refining:**

```
ELECTROLYTIC REFINING OF COPPER

    Anode (+)          Cathode (-)
    Impure Cu          Pure Cu
        │                  │
        │    CuSO₄         │
        │    Solution      │
        └────────┬─────────┘
                 │
            Electric Current
    
Anode: Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ (dissolves)
Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (deposits)

Impurities settle as anode mud
```

---

#### Corrosion and Prevention:

**Corrosion:**
- Gradual destruction of metals by reaction with air, moisture
- Example: Rusting of iron

**Rusting of Iron:**
$$ 4\text{Fe} + 3\text{O}_2 + 2x\text{H}_2\text{O} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 \cdot x\text{H}_2\text{O} $$
(Hydrated iron(III) oxide - reddish brown)

**Conditions for Rusting:**
- Presence of oxygen (air)
- Presence of moisture (water)

**Prevention Methods:**

| Method | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Painting/Greasing** | Coating to prevent air/moisture contact | Iron gates, machinery |
| **Galvanization** | Coating with zinc | Iron sheets, nails |
| **Electroplating** | Coating with Cr, Ni by electricity | Car parts, taps |
| **Alloying** | Mixing with other metals | Stainless steel |
| **Anodizing** | Thick oxide layer formation | Aluminium utensils |

### 3.5 Ionic Compounds

#### Formation of Ionic Bonds:

Ionic bond is formed by **transfer of electrons** from metal to non-metal.

**Example - Formation of NaCl:**

```
SODIUM ATOM (Na)        CHLORINE ATOM (Cl)
Electron configuration: 2, 8, 1    2, 8, 7
       ●                       ●●●●●●●
      ●●●                     ●●●●●●●
     ●●●●●    loses 1e⁻       ●●●●●●●  gains 1e⁻
      ●●●     ─────────→      ●●●●●●●
       ●                       ●●●●●●●
    
    Na⁺                        Cl⁻
(2, 8) - Stable             (2, 8, 8) - Stable

    Na⁺  +  Cl⁻  →  NaCl (Ionic compound)
```

**Examples of Ionic Compounds:**

| Compound | Formula | Ions Present |
|----------|---------|--------------|
| Sodium chloride | NaCl | Na⁺, Cl⁻ |
| Magnesium chloride | MgCl₂ | Mg²⁺, Cl⁻ |
| Calcium oxide | CaO | Ca²⁺, O²⁻ |
| Aluminium oxide | Al₂O₃ | Al³⁺, O²⁻ |

---

#### Properties of Ionic Compounds:

| Property | Explanation |
|----------|-------------|
| **Physical Nature** | Hard and brittle (strong ionic bonds) |
| **Melting/Boiling Point** | High (strong electrostatic forces) |
| **Solubility** | Soluble in water, insoluble in organic solvents |
| **Conductivity** | Conducts in molten/aqueous state (ions free to move) |
| **Crystal Structure** | Regular crystal lattice arrangement |

```
IONIC LATTICE STRUCTURE (NaCl)

    Na⁺   Cl⁻   Na⁺
      ●───○───●
      │   │   │
    Cl⁻───Na⁺───Cl⁻
      ○───●───○
      │   │   │
    Na⁺───Cl⁻───Na⁺
      ●───○───●

Alternating positive and negative ions
Strong electrostatic attraction
```

## 4. Guided Examples

### Example 1: Predicting Displacement Reactions

**Question:** Will the following reactions occur? Give reason.
(i) Zn + CuSO₄ → ?
(ii) Cu + ZnSO₄ → ?
(iii) Fe + CuSO₄ → ?

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

| Reaction | Analysis | Will it occur? |
|----------|----------|----------------|
| (i) Zn + CuSO₄ | Zn is MORE reactive than Cu (higher in reactivity series) | ✅ Yes |
| (ii) Cu + ZnSO₄ | Cu is LESS reactive than Zn (lower in reactivity series) | ❌ No |
| (iii) Fe + CuSO₄ | Fe is MORE reactive than Cu | ✅ Yes |

**Equations:**

(i) $$ \text{Zn}_{(s)} + \text{CuSO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_{4(aq)} + \text{Cu}_{(s)} $$
(Observation: Blue colour fades, reddish-brown copper deposits)

(ii) $$ \text{Cu}_{(s)} + \text{ZnSO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{No reaction} $$

(iii) $$ \text{Fe}_{(s)} + \text{CuSO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{FeSO}_{4(aq)} + \text{Cu}_{(s)} $$
(Observation: Blue colour changes to green, reddish-brown copper deposits)

### Example 2: Balancing Metal Reactions

**Question:** Balance the following equations:
(i) Na + H₂O → NaOH + H₂
(ii) Al + H₂SO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + H₂
(iii) Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

**(i) Sodium with water:**

| Step | Action | Equation |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Balance Na | 2Na + H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂ |
| 2 | Balance H | 2Na + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + H₂ |
| 3 | Check O | ✅ Balanced (2 O on both sides) |

**Balanced:** $$ 2\text{Na}_{(s)} + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(l)} \rightarrow 2\text{NaOH}_{(aq)} + \text{H}_{2(g)} $$

**(ii) Aluminium with sulphuric acid:**

| Step | Action | Equation |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Balance Al | 2Al + H₂SO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + H₂ |
| 2 | Balance SO₄ | 2Al + 3H₂SO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + H₂ |
| 3 | Balance H | 2Al + 3H₂SO₄ → Al₂(SO₄)₃ + 3H₂ |

**Balanced:** $$ 2\text{Al}_{(s)} + 3\text{H}_2\text{SO}_{4(aq)} \rightarrow \text{Al}_2(\text{SO}_4)_{3(aq)} + 3\text{H}_{2(g)} $$

**(iii) Iron with steam:**

**Balanced:** $$ 3\text{Fe}_{(s)} + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}_{(g)} \rightarrow \text{Fe}_3\text{O}_{4(s)} + 4\text{H}_{2(g)} $$

### Example 3: Electronic Configuration and Valency

**Question:** Write the electronic configuration and valency of:
(i) Sodium (Atomic number 11)
(ii) Oxygen (Atomic number 8)
(iii) Magnesium (Atomic number 12)

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

| Element | Atomic Number | Electronic Configuration | Valency |
|---------|---------------|-------------------------|---------|
| (i) Sodium | 11 | 2, 8, 1 | +1 (loses 1 electron) |
| (ii) Oxygen | 8 | 2, 6 | -2 (gains 2 electrons) |
| (iii) Magnesium | 12 | 2, 8, 2 | +2 (loses 2 electrons) |

**Ion Formation:**

- Na → Na⁺ + e⁻
- O + 2e⁻ → O²⁻
- Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻

### Example 4: Formation of Ionic Compound

**Question:** Explain the formation of magnesium chloride (MgCl₂) with electron dot structures.

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

```
MAGNESIUM ATOM (Mg)         CHLORINE ATOM (Cl)
Configuration: 2, 8, 2      Configuration: 2, 8, 7

    Mg:  ··              Cl:  ··
         ··                   ··
                              ··

Mg loses 2 electrons        Each Cl gains 1 electron

    Mg²⁺                    Cl⁻
  [2, 8]                  [2, 8, 8]

One Mg²⁺ combines with two Cl⁻

    Mg²⁺  +  2Cl⁻  →  MgCl₂
```

**Electron Dot Structure:**

```
    ··        ··              ··  ··
Mg: ··   +  2 :Cl:   →   [Mg]²⁺ 2[:Cl:]⁻
          ··                    ··
```

### Example 5: Extraction Process

**Question:** Explain how zinc is extracted from its ore (zinc blende, ZnS).

**Step-by-Step Solution:**

| Step | Process | Equation |
|------|---------|----------|
| 1 | **Concentration** | Remove gangue by froth flotation |
| 2 | **Roasting** | Heat ZnS in excess air |
| | | $$ 2\text{ZnS} + 3\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{ZnO} + 2\text{SO}_2 $$ |
| 3 | **Reduction** | Heat ZnO with coke |
| | | $$ \text{ZnO} + \text{C} \rightarrow \text{Zn} + \text{CO} $$ |
| 4 | **Refining** | Purify by distillation |

**Flow Chart:**

```
Zinc Blende (ZnS)
      ↓ (Concentration)
Concentrated Ore
      ↓ (Roasting at 900°C)
Zinc Oxide (ZnO)
      ↓ (Reduction with Coke at 1400°C)
Impure Zinc
      ↓ (Distillation)
Pure Zinc (99.98%)
```

**Smelting:**
$$ 2\text{Cu}_2\text{S} + 3\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{Cu}_2\text{O} + 2\text{SO}_2 $$
$$ 2\text{Cu}_2\text{O} + \text{Cu}_2\text{S} \rightarrow 6\text{Cu} + \text{SO}_2 $$

**(c) Refining:**
- Electrolytic refining
- Impure Cu as anode
- Pure Cu deposits on cathode
</details>

**Q64.** (a) What is the difference between a mineral and an ore?
(b) Explain the enrichment of ore.
(c) Why is flux added during smelting?

<details>
<summary>✓ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer:**

**(a) Differences:**

| Mineral | Ore |
|---------|-----|
| Natural element/compound in crust | Mineral from which metal extracted |
| May or may not be economical | Economically viable |
| All ores are minerals | All minerals are not ores |
| Example: Clay (Al mineral) | Example: Bauxite (Al ore) |

**(b) Enrichment of Ore:**

**Purpose:** Remove gangue (impurities)

**Methods:**
1. **Hydraulic washing** - Based on density
2. **Magnetic separation** - Magnetic properties
3. **Froth flotation** - For sulphide ores
4. **Leaching** - Chemical method

**(c) Flux:**

**Purpose:** Remove remaining impurities

**Action:**
- Reacts with gangue to form slag
- Slag floats, easily removed

**Example:**
$$ \text{CaO (flux)} + \text{SiO}_2 \text{ (gangue)} \rightarrow \text{CaSiO}_3 \text{ (slag)} $$
</details>

**Q65.** (a) Explain the following terms with examples:
(i) Mineral
(ii) Ore
(iii) Gangue
(iv) Flux
(v) Slag

(b) Draw a flow chart for extraction of iron from haematite.

<details>
<summary>✓ Show Answer</summary>

**Answer:**

**(a) Terms:**

**(i) Mineral:**
- Natural element/compound in earth's crust
- Example: Bauxite, Haematite

**(ii) Ore:**
- Mineral for economical metal extraction
- Example: Bauxite (Al), Haematite (Fe)

**(iii) Gangue:**
- Earthly impurities in ore
- Example: Sand, clay, soil

**(iv) Flux:**
- Substance added to remove gangue
- Example: Limestone (CaCO₃)

**(v) Slag:**
- Fusible product from flux + gangue
- Example: CaSiO₃

**(b) Iron Extraction Flow Chart:**

```
Haematite Ore (Fe₂O₃)
        ↓
Concentration (Hydraulic washing)
        ↓
Concentrated Ore + Coke + Limestone
        ↓
Blast Furnace (1500°C)
        ↓
    ┌───────┴───────┐
    ↓               ↓
Molten Iron    Slag (CaSiO₃)
(Pig Iron)     (Removed)
        ↓
Refining
        ↓
Steel/Cast Iron
```

**Reactions in Blast Furnace:**
$$ \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 + 3\text{CO} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe} + 3\text{CO}_2 $$
$$ \text{CaCO}_3 \rightarrow \text{CaO} + \text{CO}_2 $$
$$ \text{CaO} + \text{SiO}_2 \rightarrow \text{CaSiO}_3 $$
</details>
